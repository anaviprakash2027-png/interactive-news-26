Evanston residents seek improved cycling accessiblity through city Bike Plan update

By Anavi Prakash

Evanston’s Mack’s Bike & Goods was one of several hosts of a “We Ride in Unity” event across the nation on Jan. 31, leading a bike ride protesting the shooting of Alex Pretti by Customs and Border Protection agents in Minneapolis earlier that week.

Despite the rain and frigid temperatures that Saturday, over 50 riders attended, store co-owner Kelly Mack said.

The group stopped at Lighthouse Beach, where they honored the people who had been killed by federal agents until that point, Mack said. She added there was time for people to share what motivated them to attend as well as talk about “what’s happening on a larger scale to our communities.”

“It felt like, truly, a moment of unity in so many different ways,” Mack said.

She said for Evanston cyclists, their big picture conversations included talking about the city’s Bike Plan Update, set to launch in 2026.

After the most recent update to its Bike Plan in 2014, the city’s Public Works Department is now undertaking an upgrade to the plan. The update aims to address gaps in current bicycle infrastructure as well as areas around Evanston with “challenging conditions,” according to the city’s website.

As a 6th Ward resident since 2012, Mack said she’s seen people bike around the city for all sorts of reasons: as their primary mode of transportation, for exercise and to create memorable family experiences.

“I think the desire to ride bicycles is there and the willingness to give it a shot,” she said. “I do think there's a truly authentic hesitation from a parent's perspective, around their school age children navigating the city streets in a way that they are safe.”

She said there is “so much potential” within the Bike Plan update to make it easier for parents to teach their children how to ride in Evanston on bikes. As her son learns how to navigate the city on his bicycle, she said she has to explain when to be on the sidewalk, how to avoid pedestrians and when to be in the bike lane compared to the road.

McCormick first-year Andrew Hunt has similar memories of learning how to ride a bike in St. Louis. Having several cyclists in his family, including his dad, Hunt learned the skill when he was five years old. By middle school, he could ride without using his hands.

For him, Evanston’s bike paths are already much better than the ones at home because the paths — especially those going along Lake Michigan — are more continuous than what he is used to, and they have clearer signage.

The bike path along Sheridan Road and Northwestern’s campus is especially helpful, but still creates the occasional hardship, he said.

“Sometimes I have to go across, get to the other side of Sheridan and then you have to find a little exit so you can get out of this meeting, or you have to go onto the sidewalk, and you might get in people's way,” Hunt said.

He added that his ideal bike infrastructure would involve redesigning roads to be “slower moving” and aimed at smaller vehicles.

Bike Plan update seeks to revise 12 year old goals

For its Bike Plan update, the city plans to use millions in funding from the Illinois Transportation Enhancement Program and the Chicago Metropolitan Agency for Planning to improve biking conditions on Chicago Avenue and Church Street.

The Church Street Pedestrian and Bicycle Improvements project, set to begin in 2027, will add two-way bike lanes on the street from Dodge Avenue to the city’s western border as well as create a shared use trail from Church Street to Harbert Park.

The Chicago Avenue Multimodal Corridor Improvements project, slated to begin in 2028, focuses on the area between Howard Street and Davis Street on Chicago Avenue, where two-way protected bike lanes will be added.

Photo courtesy of the City of Evanston.

The last Bike Plan update was in 2014 and drove improvement projects until 2022. Some of the largest improvements occurred on Chicago Avenue, near Northwestern University on Sheridan Road and near Evanston Township High School on Dodge Avenue.

“This year's Bike Plan Update is a generational opportunity to build on this progress with the goal of a comprehensive bike network for all ages and abilities. We especially want older, younger, and less-confident bicyclists in Evanston to see cycling as a safe and viable transportation option,” Evanston Transit Alliance Communications Chair Robert Keding wrote in response to a request for comment.

Keding also encouraged Northwestern community members, including those who commute to the University, to provide their feedback on the plan.

Community feedback sought through community meetings, online portal

As part of its data and feedback gathering, the city held a Bike the Ridge event in September, where residents could go on a community bike ride and provide feedback on the update to the Bike Plan. Then, in October, the city mobilized volunteers to stand at frequented intersections around the city to count how many bikes went by in a given timeframe.

In January and February, some councilmembers hosted joint ward meetings for residents to provide feedback on the new plan. Wards 1, 6 and 7 will have a feedback session on March 3.

The city also has an online portal where residents have submitted over 600 comments on where improvements can be made. The portal will close on March 15. Comments associated with a specific location in Evanston fall under the following categories:

  • Location Is Stressful or Uncomfortable for Biking
  • Bicycling / Pedestrian Improvement Opportunity
  • Safety Issue
  • Positive Feedback / Works Well
  • Maintenance or Operational Issue
  • Bicycle Parking Needed
  • Other/General Comment

One large area of concern for residents is Main Street, which has one east-west corridor.

“It’s an old street, and it’s not wide enough,” Evanston resident Mike Moran said at a ward meeting. “We don’t want to mow down all the trees, so that’s a problem.”

Mack echoed Moran’s sentiment and said traveling east and west in Evanston proves difficult on a bike unless the rider is experienced. Going north and south, however, is much easier, with protected bike lanes on Sheridan Road and by the lakefront, she said.

Mack said her most common east-west corridor is riding from Brother K Coffee to the lakefront on Lincoln Street and Harrison Street. Along the route, the bike path "suddenly just ends,” something she said she and her husband are now used to. But, having a clear bike path on the two streets would make life a lot easier and safer, she said.

The ETA has also advocated for protected bike lanes on what it calls the Lincoln-Harrison Greenway, which goes all the way from Westfield Old Orchard to the lakefront bike trail. The organization’s proposal calls for lanes in areas of heavy traffic as well as improvements to lane markings and signage.

Another area of concern is on Dodge Avenue, where Evanston Township High School is located. The city last updated its bike infrastructure in the area in 2015.

“I’m a junior in high school. I take that bike lane to school every day. I’m not sure if I would bike if that wasn’t there,” ETHS student Olin Wilson-Thomas said at a meeting. “In the past couple of months, I kept tally, there were 57 cars parked in that Dodge (Avenue) bike lane, illegally, no enforcement, no nothing. We need some concrete protection.”

For Hunt, some Evanston roads remind him of St. Louis — he isn’t sure where cyclists belong and the bike lanes are taken up by parked cars.

But, that isn’t always the case.

“What was really different about it, as compared to St. Louis, was I could always know that I was coming up on the bike trail soon, or I knew which direction I was going, because there's signs all over the place that are explicitly meant for cyclists that say you're headed toward Rogers Park, you're headed toward the lake, you're headed towards Evanston, so that's really helpful,” he said.

In terms of next steps, the city plans to begin reviewing data and community input to finalize its next course of action.
